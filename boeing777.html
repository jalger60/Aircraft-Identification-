Boeing 777: A Comprehensive Overview

The Boeing 777, a long-range, wide-body twin-engine jetliner, first entered commercial service with United Airlines on June 7, 1995. It holds the distinction of being the world's largest twinjet and was the first commercial aircraft to be designed entirely by computer.

Major Accidents

  • British Airways Flight 38 (Jan 17, 2008): A 777-200ER crash-landed short of the runway at London Heathrow due to ice crystals in the fuel system. No fatalities.
  • EgyptAir Flight (Jul 29, 2011): A fire broke out in the cockpit of a 777-200ER while parked at the gate in Cairo. The aircraft was written off.
  • Asiana Airlines Flight 214 (Jul 6, 2013): A 777-200ER crashed on final approach to San Francisco, resulting in three fatalities.
  • Malaysia Airlines Flight MH370 (Mar 8, 2014): A 777-200ER disappeared from radar, its fate remains a mystery.
  • Malaysia Airlines Flight MH17 (Jul 17, 2014): A 777-200ER was shot down over eastern Ukraine. All 298 people on board were killed.
  • Emirates Flight 521 (Aug 3, 2016): A 777-300 crash-landed at Dubai. All passengers and crew evacuated safely, but a firefighter was killed.
  • United Airlines Flight 328 (Feb 20, 2021): A 777-200 suffered an uncontained engine failure after takeoff from Denver, landing safely with no injuries.

Variants & Airline Use

The 777 family includes classic models, longer-range models, and the newest 777X generation. Major operators include Emirates, United Airlines, Cathay Pacific, and Air France, making it one of the most common long-haul aircraft in the world.

  • 777-200: The initial model from 1995.
  • 777-200ER: Extended-range version, very popular.
  • 777-300: Stretched version for more capacity.
  • 777-200LR: "Longer Range" variant for ultra-long-haul routes.
  • 777-300ER: The most successful variant, combining range and capacity.
  • 777 Freighter: A dedicated cargo version.
  • 777X: The next generation, featuring new engines and folding wingtips.
